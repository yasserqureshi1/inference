Seeing the Sines

How do computers calculate trigonometric functions? In this article, I will describe a very interesting algorithm that computers use to do this!

07 Apr 2022 · 3 min read
Share this

Before computers, we used tables. These tables were filled with various angles and their returned trigonometric value. Then if we needed a particular trigonometric value from an angle, we can interpolate the expected value from those that we already know. But how do we get those values in the first place? Well, early works by Ptolemy (100-178) used angles based on the Pythagorean theorem and similar triangles to create these tables. In particular, angles such as \(30^\circ\), \(45^\circ\) and \(60^\circ\) with which the sine and cosine can be easily calculated (think about the ratio between the adjacent, opposite and hypotenuse sides).

We can also get the values for \(36^\circ\) and \(72^\circ\) using Euclid's Proposition IV.10 of his Elements. Now we have enough angles so that when we apply trigonometric identities, we can get values for mamy more angles and fill up our table for angles from \(0^\circ\) to \(90^\circ\) in steps of \(3^\circ\). And if we need angles that are between these values, we just interpolate.

So how do computers calculate trigonometric functions? Well, they could store these tables, but that wouldn't be as accurate. Instead one common method is the use of something called the CORDIC algorithm...

CORDIC Algorithm

The CORDIC algorithm (COordinate Rotation DIgital Computer) is a simple and efficient way to calculate trigonometric functions, hyperbolic functions, square roots, multiplications, divisions, exponentials and logarithms. There are two modes of operation: rotation mode and vector mode. We will focus on rotation mode.

With this algorithm, we use the unit circle. Essentially, we rotate a vector around the unit circle until the angle between the vector and the x-axis is the desired value. Then the \((x,y)\) co-ordinates of the vector's position on the unit circle is the \(cos\) and \(sin\) respectively. We can do this mathematically as follows:

We start with a vector, \(v_0\): $$ v_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}$$ We rotate this vector in decreasing step size of \(arctan(2^{-i})\) for \(i = 0, 1, 2,...\). The rotation is calculated by... $$ v_{i+1} = R_i v_i $$ Where... $$ R_i = \begin{bmatrix} cos(\gamma_i) & -sin(\gamma_i) \\ sin(\gamma_i) & cos(\gamma_i) \\ \end{bmatrix} $$ Using some trigonometric identities... $$ cos(\gamma_i) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+tan^2(\gamma_i)}} \quad sin(\gamma_i) = \frac{tan(\gamma_i)}{\sqrt{1+tan^2(\gamma_i)}} $$ The rotation matrix becomes: $$ R_i = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+tan^2(\gamma_i)}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -tan(\gamma_i) \\ tan(\gamma_i) & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} $$ We can restrict the angles \(\gamma_i\) such that \(tan(\gamma_i) = \pm2^{-i}\). Then the multiplication with the tangent can be replaced with a division by a power of 2. Within digital computing, this can be performed with a bit shift. Our equation is now: $$ v_{i+1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+2^{-2i}}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -\sigma_i2^{-i} \\ \sigma_i2^{-i} & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} $$ Where \(\sigma_i\) is \(+1\) or \(-1\).

After a few iterations, the vector's angle will get close to our desired angle. Note that to determine whether the rotation should be clockwise or anticlockwise, we keep track of how much the angle has been rotated at each iteration and subtract it from the desired angle. By the end, we can use the final vector \(v_n\) to get the sine and cosine of the angle where the x co-ordinate is the cosine and y co-ordinate is the sine.
